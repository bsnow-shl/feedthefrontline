5.3. Controlling the appearance of your forms

When rendering a form, the CPF does a straightforward arrangement in a table, in the order that the form elements were declared. If this isn't enough for you, you can control the form layout yourself directly in the page.

Returning to our example with name, age, and biography, the form was rendered with

<? form_start();
   form_end(); ?>

Imagine form_start() as a replacement for HTML's <form>, and form_end as replacement for </form>. To lay out the form yourself, just use your own markup between the two:

<l? form_start(); ?>
Enter your name: @@name@@

<p/>Enter your age: @@your_biography@@.

<p/>Enter your biography:<br/>
@@your_biography@@.

<p/>Then click here: @@submit@@
<? form_end(); ?>

As you can see, the form engine takes your form variable names between pairs of @@ signs, and substitutes the HTML entities that correspond to your form widget declarations.

Now I wonder, what happens if you add a new form widget, but no corresponding layout for it?

        favourite_colour => array(
                'type'=>'text',
                'menu'=>array('r'=>'Red','b'=>'Blue')
        ),

The answer is this: // screen shot

What happened is that the form engine recognized you didn't drawn that favourite_colour widget, and so it tacked it on to the end of your form, for lack of a better place to put it. It's hideous, but maybe that's a good way to get you to properly lay it out. It basically forces you to keep your form definitions and page layout in sync.

Note: You can use PHP code between the start() and end() calls.
